DAHI BAINGAN

Dahi Baingan is a flavorful Indian dish made by combining fried brinjal (eggplant) slices with a spiced yogurt sauce and aromatic tempering. To prepare it, brinjals are sliced and shallow-fried until golden and soft after being lightly seasoned with turmeric, red chili powder, and salt. Meanwhile, thick curd (yogurt) is whisked smooth and mixed with roasted cumin powder, a pinch of sugar (optional), and salt to taste. The yogurt can be thinned slightly with water to achieve a pourable consistency. The fried brinjal slices are then layered over the yogurt in a serving dish. A hot tempering is prepared using mustard seeds, cumin seeds, dried red chilies, curry leaves, green chili (optional), and a pinch of asafoetida (hing), all sizzled in oil or ghee. This fragrant tempering is poured over the yogurt and brinjal just before serving. Dahi Baingan can be served chilled or at room temperature and pairs well with plain rice, jeera rice, or rotis. The cool yogurt, crispy brinjal, and spicy tempering make this a delicious and balanced dish perfect for hot weather or festive meals.



KHICHUDI

Khechudi (also spelled Khichdi) is a comforting one-pot dish made from rice and lentils, popular in Odia and other Indian cuisines. To prepare it, wash and soak 1 cup of rice and ½ cup of moong dal (yellow split lentils) for about 15–20 minutes. In a heavy-bottomed pot or pressure cooker, heat 2–3 tablespoons of ghee or oil. Add whole spices like bay leaf, cumin seeds, cinnamon stick, cloves, and cardamom. Once aromatic, add chopped ginger and optionally green chilies. Sauté for a minute, then add the drained rice and dal mixture. Stir everything for a couple of minutes, then add about 3–3.5 cups of water, salt, and turmeric powder. Cover and cook until the rice and dal are soft and slightly mushy. In Odia-style khechudi, vegetables like diced potatoes, carrots, and green peas are often added, especially on festive days. You can also prepare a simple tempering of ghee and cumin seeds with a pinch of asafoetida (hing) and pour it on top before serving. Garnish with fried cashews or fresh coriander leaves if desired. Khechudi is commonly served with dalma (vegetable dal), raita, or tomato khatta. It’s not only nutritious and easy to digest but also holds cultural importance as a temple offering or festive food in Odisha.



DALMA

Dalma is a traditional Odia dish made by cooking lentils (usually arhar/toor dal) with a mix of vegetables like raw banana, pumpkin, papaya, eggplant, and beans. The dal and veggies are pressure-cooked together with turmeric, salt, and a bit of grated ginger until soft. In a separate tempering, ghee or oil is heated with panch phoron (a mix of mustard, cumin, fennel, fenugreek, and nigella seeds), dry red chilies, and bay leaf, then added to the cooked dal. Roasted cumin and dry red chili powder (jeera-lanka gunda) is sprinkled on top for an earthy, smoky flavor. Dalma is hearty, nutritious, and typically served with rice or khechudi, making it a staple in Odia households and festive meals.



PAKHALA BHATA

Pakhala Bhata is a traditional Odia dish made by soaking cooked rice in water, often fermented slightly, and served chilled. To prepare it, plain rice is cooked and then allowed to cool; water is added (about 2–3 times the amount of rice), and the mixture is left to sit for a few hours or overnight to develop a light sour flavor. It is usually seasoned with a simple tempering of mustard seeds, curry leaves, dry red chilies, and garlic fried in mustard oil or ghee, then poured over the rice. Sometimes curd is added for a richer taste. Pakhala is typically enjoyed with sides like fried or mashed vegetables, badi chura, roasted fish, or sautéed greens, making it a cooling and wholesome meal, especially during hot summer days



CHENA PODA

Chhena Poda is a classic Odia dessert made by baking sweetened chhena (fresh cottage cheese) until it caramelizes and forms a rich, golden crust. To prepare it, fresh chhena is kneaded until smooth and then mixed with sugar, a little semolina (suji) for binding, cardamom powder, and chopped nuts like cashews and raisins. The mixture is poured into a greased and optionally caramel-lined baking dish or banana leaf-lined tin. It is then baked either in an oven or on low flame until the top turns dark brown and the inside is set. Once cooled, it is cut into pieces and served. With its smoky flavor, moist texture, and caramelized crust, Chhena Poda is fondly known as Odisha’s “burnt cheesecake.



ADISA PITHA

Adisa Pitha, a traditional sweet from Odisha, is often made during festivals or special occasions. The main ingredients are rice flour, jaggery (or sugar), and grated coconut. To prepare, rice is soaked, dried, and ground into a fine flour. In a pan, jaggery is melted with a little water, then grated coconut and cardamom powder are added to make a sticky, sweet filling. Meanwhile, the rice flour is lightly roasted and then mixed with hot water to form a soft dough. Small portions of the dough are taken, flattened into discs, filled with the coconut-jaggery mixture, and then sealed carefully like dumplings. Traditionally, Adisa Pitha is deep-fried in ghee or oil until golden brown and slightly crisp on the outside. The result is a rich, aromatic sweet with a soft interior and flavorful filling. Some variations involve steaming instead of frying for a healthier version. Adisa Pitha is cherished for its rustic charm, simplicity, and delicious taste, often enjoyed with a cup of tea or served as part of festive platters in Odia households.

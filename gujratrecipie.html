Dhokla (Steamed Gram Flour Cake)

Dhokla is a light and spongy savory cake made from gram flour (besan). To prepare, mix 1 cup of besan with 1 tbsp semolina (sooji), 1 tsp sugar, 1 tsp lemon juice, salt, and water to form a smooth batter. Add 1 tsp of fruit salt (Eno) just before steaming and mix gently. Pour the batter into a greased plate or thali and steam for 15–20 minutes until a toothpick comes out clean. For the tempering, heat oil and add mustard seeds, green chilies, curry leaves, and sesame seeds. Pour this over the cooled dhokla and garnish with chopped coriander and grated coconut. Dhokla is best served with green chutney and sweet tamarind chutney, offering a perfect mix of soft texture and tangy flavor.



Undhiyu (Mixed Vegetable Curry)

Undhiyu is a traditional Gujarati mixed vegetable dish, especially popular during winter. It includes vegetables like surti papdi (flat beans), baby potatoes, yam, brinjal, and sweet potatoes. These are marinated with a masala made of fresh coriander, grated coconut, ginger, green chilies, garlic, sesame seeds, and crushed peanuts, all mixed with lemon juice and spices. The vegetables are layered in a pot and slow-cooked with minimal water, often sealed with dough to lock in the flavor. Sometimes, methi muthiyas (fried fenugreek dumplings) are added for texture. The dish is cooked on low flame or in a pressure cooker until everything is tender and well-coated in the masala. Undhiyu is traditionally served with puris and shrikhand, making it a festive centerpiece during occasions like Uttarayan (kite festival).



Khandvi (Gram Flour Rolls)

Khandvi is a delicate, bite-sized snack made from gram flour and yogurt. To prepare, mix 1 cup besan with 1 cup sour curd, 2 cups water, turmeric, and salt. Cook this mixture over medium heat while stirring continuously until it thickens. Quickly spread the batter in a thin layer on a greased plate or back of a thali before it cools. Once firm, cut into strips and roll them tightly. For tempering, heat oil, add mustard seeds, sesame seeds, and curry leaves, then pour over the rolls. Garnish with grated coconut and chopped coriander. Khandvi is known for its melt-in-the-mouth texture and mildly tangy flavor, making it a popular tea-time or party snack in Gujarat



Pakhala Bhata is a traditional Odia dish made by soaking cooked rice in water, often fermented slightly, and served chilled. To prepare it, plain rice is cooked and then allowed to cool; water is added (about 2–3 times the amount of rice), and the mixture is left to sit for a few hours or overnight to develop a light sour flavor. It is usually seasoned with a simple tempering of mustard seeds, curry leaves, dry red chilies, and garlic fried in mustard oil or ghee, then poured over the rice. Sometimes curd is added for a richer taste. Pakhala is typically enjoyed with sides like fried or mashed vegetables, badi chura, roasted fish, or sautéed greens, making it a cooling and wholesome meal, especially during hot summer days



Handvo (Savory Lentil Cake)

Handvo is a nutritious savory cake made from a fermented batter of rice and mixed lentils like toor dal, chana dal, and urad dal. Soak and grind the ingredients into a thick batter, then ferment overnight. To the fermented batter, add grated bottle gourd, curd, green chilies, ginger, turmeric, baking soda, and salt. Pour the batter into a greased pan with a tempering of mustard seeds, sesame seeds, and curry leaves. Cook covered on low flame or bake until the crust turns golden and crispy. Handvo is crispy on the outside and soft inside, packed with flavor and nutrition. Often enjoyed as a breakfast or evening snack, it's served with chutney or pickle



Sev Tameta Nu Shaak (Tomato Curry with Sev)

Sev Tameta nu Shaak is a sweet, spicy, and tangy curry made from tomatoes and garnished with crispy sev (fried gram flour noodles). To make it, sauté mustard seeds, cumin seeds, and green chilies in oil, then add chopped tomatoes. Cook until soft and pulpy. Add turmeric, red chili powder, coriander powder, jaggery, and salt. Simmer the curry until it thickens slightly. Just before serving, top the hot curry with plenty of sev and chopped coriander. Serve with rotis or theplas. The contrast of soft tangy tomatoes and crunchy sev gives this dish its unique appeal, making it a comfort food favorite in Gujarati homes.
